Page 2: Introduction

This project gives a new, happy ending to the classic story of Little Red Riding Hood. Instead of ending in fear, it ends with friendship, kindness, and lessons on how people can change for the better. Through colorful narration and visuals, it inspires children to believe in second chances.

Page 3: Original Story Overview

The original story of Little Red Riding Hood is about a girl who travels through the forest to deliver food to her grandmother. On the way, she meets a cunning wolf who tricks her and eats her grandmother. In some versions, Red is also eaten, and in others, a woodcutter saves them both.

Page 4: Why Reimagine the Ending?

The original ending of the story can be frightening for young readers. By changing the ending to something happier and more positive, children learn that mistakes can be fixed and that even bad characters can learn to do better.

Page 5: New Story – The Beginning

Red was walking through the forest happily, humming a song. She was taking cookies and juice to her grandmother. On the way, she met the same wolf. But this time, something was different. The wolf looked sad and tired, not scary.

Page 6: The Wolf’s Change

When Red met the wolf, he didn't try to eat her. Instead, he asked, “Why are you always so kind?” Red smiled and said, “Because the world becomes better with kindness.” The wolf paused. For the first time, someone was kind to him.

Page 7: Red’s Grandmother

Red and the wolf reached her grandmother's house. Grandmother was surprised to see the wolf but didn't panic. She served tea and listened to the wolf’s story. He told them that he had no friends and was lonely. Grandmother hugged him warmly.

Page 8: A New Friendship

From that day, the wolf promised to be good. He helped Red collect flowers, fix grandma's fence, and even plant vegetables. The villagers, at first afraid, saw the change and began inviting him to festivals. Red and the wolf became best friends.

Page 9: Character Sketch – Red

Red is a sweet, curious, and loving girl. She never judges anyone quickly and always helps others. She believes in second chances and shows that even young children can teach adults to be kind.

Page 10: Character Sketch – The Wolf

The wolf was once feared by all. But deep down, he was lonely and misunderstood. With care and compassion, he became a helpful, kind-hearted friend loved by everyone in the village.

Page 11–20: Moral Lessons

  • Kindness changes lives.
  • Never judge a book by its cover.
  • Even scary characters can become heroes.
  • Friendship can heal old wounds.
  • Adults can learn from children too.
  • It’s okay to forgive mistakes.
  • Love and warmth matter more than fear.
  • Loneliness makes people act differently.
  • Change begins with one act of kindness.
  • There’s good in everyone.

Page 22–24: Reflection Questions

  1. Why did the wolf change in this story?
  2. How did Red help him become better?
  3. What lesson does this story teach us?
  4. Can you think of a time when kindness helped you?
  5. How would you rewrite other stories with better endings?

Page 25: Conclusion

This reimagined story shows us that fairy tales don’t always need to end in sadness or fear. With a little change in thinking, we can make stories more positive and inspiring for young minds. Red Riding Hood and the wolf now walk through the forest, not as enemies, but as true friends.
